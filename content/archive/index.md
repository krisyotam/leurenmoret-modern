**New Vaccines against infectious and non-infectious diseases.**  
There are many vaccines for both infectious diseases and non-infectious diseases that are either undergoing licensure or are in advanced clinical trials. This list is dynamic as products move from early trials through to phase 3 trials and then await licensure for release onto the market. Diseases for which vaccines already exist are also subject to new vaccine technologies and there are better vaccines emerging for these. Below are some examples of emerging vaccines - some are already becoming available.  
  
**Enteric infections:  
** • Enterotoxigenic E. Coli (ETEC). Several approaches including recombinant, live attenuated, edible.  
• Rotavirus – now available  
• Shigella – live attenuated and conjugated vaccines are in trials  
  
**Herpes virus infections  
** • Live attenuated, recombinant subunits, peptides and purified DNA all show promise.  
• Cytomegalovirus – live attenuated and subunit.  
• Varicella Zoster – preventing zoster in the elderly has been demonstrated.  
• Epstein Barr virus – subunit and live recombinant vectors.  
  
**HIV  
** • Many approaches including DNA are in clinical trials.  
  
**Parasitic and tropical infections  
** • Malaria – DNA vaccines are showing promise.  
• Shistosomiasis – subunit and DNA candidates.  
  
**Respiratory Infections  
** • Group A Streptococci – recombinant subunit, several options are in trials.  
• Group B Streptococci – Conjugates  
• Meningococcal disease – vaccines targeting all pathogenic strains are in trials.  
• Respiratory syncytial virus – subunit vaccines are in clinical trials. Live attenuated are also possible.  
  
**Sexually transmitted diseases  
** • Genital herpes – subunit and live attenuated are in trials.  
• Human Papillomavirus (HPV) – Recombinant polypeptide now available.  
  


**New Technologies:**

Perhaps the approach that is generating the most interest and excitement is the technique of injecting engineered DNA sequences from infectious organisms against which protection is desired. Once delivered into the host, the carrier organism (and hence the inserted DNA) undergoes limited replication, the protein of interest is produced and the host develops an immune response against the protein. Naked DNA has also been used in trials with some success. Target organisms currently include: HIV, Helicobacter pylori, mucosal candidiasis, herpes viruses, and Human Papalloma Virus (HPV). Hepatits B is looking very promising and human clinical trials have taken place.

Advances in genomics enabled rapid progress to be made in vaccine development, which until now involved painstaking trial and error. Candidates showing promise include: S.pneumoniae, influenza and meningococcus (all strains).

**Reverse Genetics (or Reverse Vaccinology):  
** By knowing the genetic code of an organism it is possible to extrapolate the codes for all genes and therefore the proteins that they code for. Some of these will include peptides with antigenic properties. For example, this technique is being applied in the development of a meningococcal vaccine that would be protective against all strains. The search for a vaccine against all strains of this disease has been going for 40 years with little success. The main reason for failure has been the sequence and antigenic variability of the antigens which have been identified during this period using conventional methods. Recently the genome for Nesseria meningitidis was sequenced.  
  
• In reverse vaccinology the genome sequence can be fed into a computer, which can predict the proteins located in the bacterium’s surface.  
• Using this method for meningococcal there were 600 predicted proteins identified in silico (computational biology).  
• Of these, 350 were successfully expressed in E.coli as fusion products, purified and used to immunise mice.  
• There were 91 novel surface proteins identified, 29 of these induced bactericidal antibodies – a prediction of a good vaccine candidate.  
• Prior to this, only 12-15 had been identified and only 4 –5 of these had bactericidal properties.  
• Vaccine candidates are in clinical trials.  
  
**Advantages in reverse vaccinology  
** • Fast access to virtually every antigen  
• Non-cultivable organisms can be approached – the whole organism does not need to be grown to produce antigens  
• Non-abundant antigens can be identified – less common antigens which may be immunogenic can be identified.  
• Antigens not expressed in vitro can be identified.  
• Non-structural proteins can be used  
  
**Disadvantages**  
• Non-proteinous antigens, like polysaccharides and glycolipids, cannot be used.  
  
This technology is currently also being applied to vaccines for pandemic influenza.

  


**DNA vaccines**

With DNA vaccines, the subject is not injected with the actual antigen but with DNA encoding the antigen. This usually involves isolating one or more genes from a disease-causing agent with known antigenic properties and splicing those genes into plasmids (which are closed rings of self replicating DNA.) The rings are then delivered into small groups of cells, often by injection into muscle cells or by propulsion into the skin via a so-called gene gun. The plasmid is taken up by the host’s cells, transcribed and expressed. The body now produces the foreign antigen.

• Successes in clinical trials in terms of safety and immunogenicity with DNA vaccines include hepatitis B and malaria.

• Difficulties include identifying the antigenic peptides, the potential to develop autoimmune responses and selection of viral vectors.  
• The DNA vaccine can be injected into a muscle just as conventional vaccines are.  
• DNA vaccines elicit a broad cell-mediated, as well as antibody-mediated immune responses.

**New vaccines against non-infectious diseases  
** • When correctly targeted, an immune response can be used to eliminate cells with pathological behavior such as dysplasia and malignancy. Alternatively, immune responses can reduce inflammation affecting a specific organ, such as in diabetes. Two of the most promising areas in this regard are vaccines against cancer and autoimmune diseases.  
• One of the most promising treatments for cancers lies in the potential of vaccines.  
• There are many approaches to cancer vaccine development, but all must overcome the uniqueness of every cancer and therefore often must be tailor-made to the patient. Some vaccines will use the tumor’s own antigens to stimulate the immune system into attacking it (demonstrated for melanoma), while others will work with the immune system, enhancing its tumor killing capabilities.  
• Vaccines have also been developed as potential therapeutic agents against nicotine and cocaine addictions.  
  
**Vaccines against biological weapons  
** Of increased interest recently is the potential of human destruction by biological weapons of mass destruction. Vaccines are likely to be the only practical means of protection. Second generation vaccines against anthrax, smallpox and plague are being developed as are vaccines against hemorrhagic fever viruses. The absence of a financially viable market for these vaccines is an impediment to their development, as are regulatory and logistical obstacles to conducting human trials.  
  
**New vaccine-delivery technology  
** Most currently recommended vaccines require parenteral administration and many require a series of injections. Alternatives are being sought and include transcutaneous vaccines, edible plants containing genes for human vaccine antigens and controlled delivery depot systems. Almost all current vaccines are injected. Considerations include the availability and cost of skilled labour to perform the injection, the cost of producing injected vaccines, and the need for cold storage. However, there are alternatives to injection as the means for vaccination; mucosal immunization is one such option.  
  
**Mucosal/Edible Vaccines  
** • The mucosa is the boundary inside the body at which it primarily deals with its environment.  
• Mucosa includes the conjunctival sac, the vaginal mucosa, the rectal mucosa, the buccal and gastrointestinal mucosa, and the nasal mucosa. To protect these surfaces, the mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue is directly on site to provide defense. If the body comes in contact with an antigen at any place on the mucosa, the antigen stimulates these cells to produce an antibody response which is reported to all other “associated mucous membranes.” These other “associated mucous membranes” include the nasal-associated lymphoid tissue, the bronchial-associated lymphoid tissue, and the gastrointestinal-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT).  
• Mucosal vaccines expose a particular antigen to the mucosal surfaces thereby allowing the immune system to produce specific antibodies against it.  
• An important point about mucosal vaccines is the different delivery strategies it offers. This is because mucosal vaccines need not be injected; rather, edible material containing the vaccine would be sufficient. If this edible material were part of the diet of many people, then the administration of such vaccines would be much easier.  
• With edible plant vaccine (EPV) technology, this is what many scientists are working towards. EPV requires antigen expression in transgenic plants and is then given by oral delivery.  
• Food vaccines are especially useful when the disease it prevents occurs in the gut. As vaccine-containing food is broken down by the digestive system, cells along the wall of the intestines are building up the immune response. Theoretically, when the antigen is detected again, antibodies will be available to attach to the antigen and destroy it.  
• A number of different edible plant vaccines are currently under development. The types of diseases targeted so far include travelers’ diarrhea, norwalk virus, and hepatitis B.  
• So far, potatoes, bananas and tomatoes have been used as vectors to deliver vaccine. Of these,the banana is ideal because it is eaten raw, and is used commonly in tropical areas of the world as a weaning food for children.  
• Clinical trials have confirmed that these vaccines appear both safe and able to generate antibodies.

BACKGROUND:  
The deleterious impact of uranium on human health has been linked to its radioactive and heavy metal–chemical properties. Decades of research has defined the causal relationship between uranium mining/milling and onset of kidney and respiratory diseases 25 years later.  
OBJECTIVE:  
We investigated the hypothesis that uranium, similar to other heavy metals such as cadmium, acts like estrogen.  
METHODS:  
In several experiments, we exposed intact, ovariectomized, or pregnant mice to depleted uranium in drinking water [ranging from 0.5 μg/L (0.001 μM) to 28 mg/L (120 μM).  
RESULTS:  
Mice that drank uranium-containing water exhibited estrogenic responses including selective reduction of primary follicles, increased uterine weight, greater uterine luminal epithelial cell height, accelerated vaginal opening, and persistent presence of cornified vaginal cells. Coincident treatment with the antiestrogen ICI 182,780 blocked these responses to uranium or the synthetic estrogen diethylstilbestrol. In addition, mouse dams that drank uranium-containing water delivered grossly normal pups, but they had significantly fewer primordial follicles than pups whose dams drank control tap water.
